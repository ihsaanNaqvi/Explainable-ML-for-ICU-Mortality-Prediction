# ABSTRACT

**Naqvi I.** Explainable Machine Learning for Mortality Prediction in Intensive Care Units: A Time-Step SHAP Attribution Approach on the PhysioNet 2012 Challenge Dataset. — Master's thesis. — Tomsk: TSU, 2026. — 96 p.

The thesis develops a framework for predicting in-hospital mortality of intensive care unit patients from the first forty-eight hours of physiological monitoring and, more importantly, for explaining the predictions in a way clinicians can interrogate. The work uses Set-A of the PhysioNet/Computing in Cardiology Challenge 2012, which contains records of four thousand adult patients admitted to medical, surgical, coronary, and cardiac surgery units. After cleaning, the cohort exhibits an in-hospital mortality rate of 13.5 per cent, which makes the task a strongly imbalanced binary classification problem.

Three model families are compared on identical pre-processed inputs: a gradient-boosted tree model (XGBoost) built on 228 hand-engineered temporal features, a Temporal Convolutional Network with dilated causal convolutions, and a Time-Aware Transformer that uses learnable sinusoidal positional encodings and a missingness-aware input layout of seventy-four channels (thirty-seven physiological variables and thirty-seven binary masks). All models are trained with the same train/validation/test partition of 2800/600/600 patients and evaluated on the official PhysioNet 2012 metrics — Event 1 (Score1 = min(Sensitivity, Positive Predictivity) at the optimal probability threshold) and Event 2 (the Hosmer–Lemeshow H statistic). XGBoost obtains AUROC = 0.8798, Score1 = 0.5422, and HL-H = 37.67. The Transformer obtains AUROC = 0.8542 and Score1 = 0.5301. Both surpass the original PhysioNet SAPS-I baseline (Score1 = 0.3097).

The principal scientific contribution is a *time-step SHAP attribution* procedure. The standard SHAP estimator returns one importance score per flattened input feature, which destroys time information when the input is a multivariate trajectory. We compute SHAP values on the 228-dimensional flat feature vector, then redistribute each variable-level attribution across the forty-eight hourly bins in proportion to the absolute observed value, producing a 600 × 48 × 37 attribution tensor that lives in the same coordinate system as the raw observations. The procedure makes it possible to answer questions of the form "which hour of which variable mattered most for *this* patient" without retraining or running a separate explainer for every clinical question.

Clinical plausibility of the attributions is validated against two established severity scores. The Spearman correlation between SHAP–SAPS-I concordance and patient-level calibration error is statistically significant (ρ = 0.139, p = 6.3 × 10⁻⁴), and the mean SAPS-I concordance score is 0.498 (std 0.156), confirming that the model assigns importance to variables clinicians independently consider prognostic. Ablation experiments isolate the contribution of each design choice: removing the missingness channels drops Score1 by 6.6 percentage points and degrades calibration markedly, identifying the encoding of *when measurements were taken* as the single most informative architectural component — a finding consistent with the long-standing clinical intuition that informative missingness in the ICU is itself a signal.

The thesis closes with a discussion of limitations (single-centre cohort, retrospective evaluation, attribution-not-causation caveat) and a roadmap toward prospective validation on the MIMIC-IV and eICU collections.

**Keywords:** intensive care, in-hospital mortality, PhysioNet 2012 Challenge, explainable artificial intelligence, SHAP, Transformer neural network, time-series classification, missingness, clinical decision support.
